## Post Content

Most AI projects don't fail because of bad models.

They fail because nobody thought about what happens after the demo.

I've been deep in AI project work lately, and here's what I keep seeing:

Teams rush to build the "cool" part — the model, the prompt chain, the agent — but skip the boring stuff that actually matters.

Things like:

→ How does this handle bad input at 2 AM with no one watching?
→ What happens when the API you depend on changes its response format?
→ Who monitors cost when usage spikes 10x overnight?
→ How do you version control prompts the way you version control code?

The real update from my AI projects isn't about a new framework or a fancy architecture.

It's this: the gap between a working prototype and a reliable system is where 80% of the actual work lives.

And that's exactly where most projects get abandoned.

Building AI that works in production means treating it like real engineering. Error handling. Logging. Fallbacks. Testing with messy, real-world data — not clean samples.

The tagline I live by: build systems that work while you sleep.

That "while you sleep" part? It's not marketing. It's a design requirement.

If you're building AI into your product or workflow right now, I'd genuinely love to hear:

What's the one problem that surprised you most after your AI feature went live?
